EDUCATION

Science communication can be conducted from many different perspectives, so we wanted to reflect the importance of doing so by having each of our sub-teams contribute resources. Human Practices led high school workshops, Lab & Design made quizzes on important iGEM topics to onboard their new members, and Math & Modelling wrote summaries of bioinorganic chemistry and transport phenomena so that future iGEM teams can grasp these topics quicker and easier than we did! HUMAN PRACTICES

Engineering Outreach

Given the global pandemic, Waterloo iGEM was worried as to if we would be able to engage with the general public in meaningful ways. In previous years, we enjoyed working with secondary school students within our region both to gain a general public opinion on our project as well as help them engage in the world of post-secondary STEM. This year was no exception. Although virtual, we continued our tradition of education and engagement of local youth through a summer partnership with the University of Waterloo’s Catalyst program.

An initiative of the Faculty of Engineering at the University of Waterloo, Catalyst provides engaging summer programs, conferences and workshops for high school youth and educators in the Waterloo community and beyond. Waterloo iGEM worked with Catalyst to provide three workshops to secondary school students interested in pursuing further education in STEM-related programs. For more information on Catalyst, please visit their page on the University of Waterloo’s website here.

Our workshops focused on educating participants on the Engineering Design Process, how to use it, and how we apply it to an iGEM project. Furthermore, we aimed to have participants leave being more aware of their carbon footprint related to the electronics and the multidisciplinary considerations (economics, environment, etc.) within their manufacturing.

These workshops were broken into three parts: a discussion on what iGEM is and how Waterloo iGEM participates, a discussion on electronic manufacturing and our proposed solution to copper pollutants in wastewater, and an open discussion and build phase where participants worked together to propose their own solution using the Engineering Method.

Overall, we received feedback that the participants thoroughly enjoyed learning from us and engaging with our project. Furthermore, they left with a working understanding of the Engineering Design Process and we excited to apply this knowledge to the rest of their Catalyst engagements. They were also thankful to be able to continue to engage with members within STEM fields given that many were considering further educations or careers within its fields and appreciated hearing from some current students.

We would like to thank Catalyst for this incredible partnership experience and look forward to working with them in the future.

LAB & DESIGN

Team Workshops

Since the majority of the lab team’s members were new to the world of synthetic biology and iGEM, we conducted workshops about BioBricks, recombinant proteins, and protein structural modeling to introduce them to the terminology and concepts we would be utilizing in this year’s project. We thought that other iGEM teams could find these resources useful when onboarding new members as well! To make the workshops easy to share and run by other teams, we added slide notes to the PowerPoint and also built accompanying Kahoot quizzes to test members’ learning in a fun, engaging way.

In partnership with the University of Waterloo’s Engineering Society, we ran a workshop that explained to our fellow students how we employed the engineering cycle in synthetic biology to execute an iGEM project every year. Introduction to transport phenomena

The process design portion of this year’s project required many modelling tools based on transport phenomena. Transport phenomena include momentum transport, mass transport, and energy transport, all of which are critical concepts in chemical engineering. We created a series of documents providing an introduction to transport phenomena suitable for those with a basic understanding of physics and single-variable calculus.
